## NASA's Artemis Program

### What Is It?
NASA's program to return humans to the Moon and establish a sustainable presence, then use that as a stepping stone to Mars.

### The Mission Timeline

#### **Artemis II: Moon Flyby (February 2026)**
- **What:** Crewed mission to orbit the Moon and return
- **Crew:** 4 astronauts (Reid Wiseman, Victor Glover, Christina Koch, Jeremy Hansen)
- **Duration:** 10 days
- **Rocket:** NASA's SLS (Space Launch System)
- **Spacecraft:** Orion capsule
- **Goal:** Test Orion's life support and navigation systems

**This is the mission you heard about!** It goes **around** the Moon but doesn't land.

#### **Artemis III: Moon Landing (2027-2028)**
- **What:** First human lunar landing since Apollo 17 (1972)
- **Lander:** SpaceX Starship HLS
- **Location:** Near lunar south pole
- **Goal:** Land 2 astronauts on the surface for ~1 week

#### **Artemis IV & Beyond (2028+)**
- Docking with Lunar Gateway (space station orbiting Moon)
- Additional landing missions
- Building sustainable Moon presence

## SpaceX's Role: Starship HLS

### What Is HLS?
**Human Landing System** - SpaceX's Starship adapted to land astronauts on the Moon.

### How It Fits Artemis
- NASA selected SpaceX to build the lunar lander
- **Artemis III:** Starship HLS will launch separately, dock with Orion in lunar orbit, then descend to Moon
- **Artemis IV:** Starship HLS will dock with Lunar Gateway

### SpaceX Timeline (Rumored/Leaked)
- **June 2026:** Orbital refueling demonstration
- **June 2027:** Uncrewed Starship HLS lunar landing test
- **September 2028:** Crewed Artemis III landing

**Note:** SpaceX's internal timeline is more aggressive than NASA's official schedule.

## SpaceX's Mars Ambitions

### The Goal
Establish a self-sustaining city on Mars.

### Current Timeline (Elon Musk's Vision)
- **2026:** First uncrewed Starships to Mars (launch window)
- **2027:** Uncrewed landing on Mars
- **2028:** First crewed Mars mission (if tests successful)
- **2029-2031:** ~20 missions to Mars
- **2030s:** Scaling to ~100 missions per window

### How They'll Do It
1. **Launch Starship** to Earth orbit
2. **Refuel in orbit** (Starship "tanker" ships transfer fuel)
3. **Trans-Mars injection** (burn for Mars trajectory)
4. **Land on Mars** (propulsive landing using Raptors)
5. **Return** (produce methane fuel on Mars using in-situ resources)

## Rocket Technology: SLS vs Starship

### NASA's SLS (Space Launch System)

| Specification | Value |
|---------------|-------|
| **Height** | 321 feet (98m) |
| **Thrust** | 8.8 million lbs (39.1 MN) |
| **Payload to LEO** | ~130,000 kg (Block 1) |
| **Payload to Moon** | ~46 tons (Block 2) |
| **Engines** | 4 RS-25 (core) + 2 SRBs (solid boosters) |
| **Cost per launch** | ~$2-4 billion USD |
| **Reusability** | None (expendable) |
| **Development** | NASA + Boeing + other contractors |

**Key Points:**
- Most powerful rocket ever flown (as of 2022)
- Derived from Space Shuttle technology
- Very expensive, not reusable
- Designed for NASA crewed missions

---

### SpaceX Starship + Super Heavy

| Specification | Value |
|---------------|-------|
| **Height** | 394 feet (120m) with booster |
| **Thrust** | 17 million lbs (74 MN) at liftoff |
| **Payload to LEO** | 150-250 tons (fully reusable) |
| **Payload to Moon** | ~100-150 tons (with refueling) |
| **Engines** | 33 Raptor 2/3 (Super Heavy) + 6 Raptor (Starship) |
| **Cost per launch** | Target: <$10 million USD |
| **Reusability** | Fully reusable (rapid turnaround) |
| **Development** | SpaceX (private company) |

**Key Points:**
- **2x more powerful than SLS**
- Fully and rapidly reusable
- Designed for mass transit to space
- Orbital refueling enables deep space missions
- Still in testing and development

## Raptor Engines: The Next Generation

### Raptor 3 (Latest Version)
- **Thrust:** ~2.3 MN (sea level)
- **Specific impulse:** ~350 s (sea level)
- **Propellant:** Liquid methane + liquid oxygen
- **Design:** Full-flow staged combustion (very efficient)
- **Cost:** Much cheaper than traditional engines

### Why It Matters
- Highest thrust-to-weight ratio of any rocket engine ever built
- Designed for rapid reusability (can fly many times with minimal refurbishment)
- Methane fuel can be produced on Mars (in-situ resource utilization)

## Why Two Different Approaches?

### NASA's SLS Philosophy
- **Safety-first:** Proven technology, conservative design
- **Crew-focused:** Designed for human missions from day one
- **Cost not primary:** Congressionally mandated, jobs-focused
- **Reliability:** Extensive testing, high assurance

### SpaceX's Starship Philosophy
- **Rapid iteration:** Build, test, fail, improve, repeat
- **Cost-focused:** Reusability drives down launch costs by 100x
- **Mars-first:** Designed with Mars as ultimate goal
- **Scalability:** Mass production, airline-like operations

## The Bigger Picture

### How They Work Together
1. **NASA SLS** launches crew in Orion capsule
2. **Orion** docks with **SpaceX Starship HLS** in lunar orbit
3. **Starship HLS** descends to Moon surface
4. **Astronauts** explore, then return to Orion
5. **Orion** returns to Earth

### Why Both?
- NASA wanted redundancy (multiple lunar lander providers)
- SpaceX brings innovation and cost competition
- Different philosophies create complementary strengths

## Key Takeaways

1. **Artemis II (Feb 2026)** is the upcoming moon flyby mission you heard about - NASA SLS + Orion, no SpaceX involvement for this one
2. **SpaceX IS involved** in Artemis III (2027-2028) as the lunar lander (Starship HLS)
3. **Starship is 2x more powerful** than SLS (17M lbs vs 8.8M lbs thrust)
4. **Raptor 3 engines** are revolutionary - highest thrust-to-weight ratio ever
5. **SpaceX Mars timeline** is ambitious: 2026 uncrewed, 2028 crewed (if all goes well)
6. **NASA is conservative** (safe, expensive), **SpaceX is aggressive** (riskier, cheaper)
7. **They're complementary:** NASA provides funding and oversight, SpaceX provides innovation and competition
